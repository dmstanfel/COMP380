Cost

Wearable technology has both physical and social costs. Even though wearable technology has the power to decrease health care cost, it may bring some other unknown costs.

Health care costs are at a all time high. The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development found that for 30 countries the ratio between health care expenditures and gross domestic product are at a peak. Some patients may be concerned with radiation from smart wearable technologies. There was even a New York Times article warning readers about radiation exposer and cancer risks. But 3 days after the article was released the Editor filed a note saying that "Neither epidemiological nor laboratory studies have found reliable evidence of such risks (cellphone radiation and cancer risk), and there is no widely accepted theory as to how they might arise." and that the article's "headline for the article online — “Could Wearable Computers Be as Harmful as Cigarettes?” — also went too far in suggesting any such comparison."

One of the goals for wearable technology companies in the health care industry is to low financial cost for healthcare providers. The article In Value-Based Care, Telehealth’s Aim is Money Saved, Not Earned highlighted this goal after attending the Xtelligent Media’s Value-Based Care Summit last June. The keynote speaker, Dr. Randall S. Moore who is the president of Mercy Virtual argued that the goal for these companies to limit wasted resources and time instead of being a reimbursement company for healthcare providers. They want to limit the time patients spend in the hospitals and provide more care for more critical patients.

In the article Wearable Tech Is Plugging Into Health Insurance suggest that wearable technology companies can cooperate with health insurances to track and change the rate of health insurance payments by using the data provided by wearable technology companies. This would lower the health insurance payments and give consumers a larger motivation to become more active. Some companies like BP gave some of the employees a free FitBit Zip and told them that if they had at least one million steps in a year, BP will lower their healthcare premium.
