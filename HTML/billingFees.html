Billing Fees

Even though wearable technology has the power to decrease health care cost, it may bring some other unknown costs

Health care costs are at a all time high. The Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development found that for 30 countries the ratio between health care expenditures and gross domestic product are at their peak. Some patients may be concerned with radiation from smart wearable technologies. In fact, a New York Times article warned readers about radiation exposer and cancer risks. However, three days after the article was released the Editor filed a note saying that "Neither epidemiological nor laboratory studies have found reliable evidence of such risks [cellphone radiation and cancer risk], and there is no widely accepted theory as to how they might arise." and that the article's "headline for the article online — 'Could Wearable Computers Be as Harmful as Cigarettes?' — also went too far in suggesting any such comparison." Additionally, the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) provides restrictions on the levels of radiation devices are allowed to emit and keep a close on these regulations to monitor their effects on people.

One of the goals for wearable technology companies in the healthcare industry is to lower financial cost for healthcare providers. In Value-Based Care, Telehealth’s Aim is Money Saved, Not Earned highlights this goal by explaining what happeneed at the Xtelligent Media’s Value-Based Care Summit last June. The keynote speaker, Dr. Randall S. Moore, who is the president of Mercy Virtual, argued that the goal for these companies is to limit wasted resources and time instead of acting as a reimbursement company for healthcare providers. Dr. Moore hopes in the future to limit the time patients spend in hospitals and provide needed hospital care for the more critical patients.

In the article Wearable Tech Is Plugging Into Health Insurance the author suggests that wearable technology companies can cooperate with health insurance firms to track and change the rate of health insurance payments by using the data provided from wearable technology. This would ultimately lower health insurance payments and give consumers a greater motivation to become more active. For instance, recently BP gave some of its employees a free FitBit Zip and told them that if they reached at least one million steps in a year, BP would lower their healthcare premium.
